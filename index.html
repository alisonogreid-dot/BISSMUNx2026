Committee 1 · 15 Delegations

General Assembly

Strengthening accountability for war crimes

The GA is debating how the international community can strengthen mechanisms to investigate, prosecute, and deter war crimes. Expect debate on the International Criminal Court (ICC), universal jurisdiction, ad-hoc tribunals, and the tension between accountability and national sovereignty. The committee fractures along clear lines: ICC champions, ICC skeptics, and states that have themselves been accused of war crimes.

Topic Background

Why this topic, why now

War crimes accountability has resurfaced as one of the most contested debates at the UN. The ICC’s 2023 arrest warrant against President Putin, ongoing investigations into Israel/Palestine, and the Rohingya, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen conflicts have reignited fundamental questions: Who has the authority to prosecute war crimes? When does accountability serve justice, and when does it risk politicisation? Should sovereign immunity protect heads of state?

Key tensions

  • Universal jurisdiction vs. sovereignty: Can national courts prosecute crimes committed in other countries?
  • ICC legitimacy: Critics argue the Court disproportionately targets African and non-Western leaders.
  • Selective enforcement: Why are some conflicts investigated while others are not?
  • Funding and cooperation: The ICC depends on member-state cooperation to make arrests — what happens when states refuse?
  • Alternative mechanisms: Hybrid tribunals, truth commissions, and transitional justice as ICC alternatives.

Committee 2 · 8 Delegations

Economic and Social Council

The strategic weaponisation of maritime trade and borders

ECOSOC is examining the economic and humanitarian consequences when states or non-state actors use maritime chokepoints, shipping routes, and borders as instruments of coercion. Recent flashpoints include Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, Russia’s Black Sea blockade, the Strait of Hormuz, the South China Sea, and the rise of “shadow fleets” evading sanctions. The framing is economic and humanitarian — not military.

Topic Background

Why this topic, why now

Roughly 80% of global trade by volume travels by sea. When chokepoints are weaponised, the impact is global: insurance premiums spike, supply chains break, food and energy prices rise, and developing countries that depend on imports suffer disproportionately. The Houthi Red Sea attacks since 2023 reduced Suez Canal traffic by over 60%, costing Egypt billions. Russia’s Black Sea blockade threatened global food security. Iran’s Hormuz threats periodically spike oil prices.

Key tensions

  • Freedom of navigation vs. coastal-state authority: UNCLOS guarantees innocent passage, but coastal states claim security rights.
  • Sanctions enforcement vs. evasion: Shadow fleets, ship-to-ship transfers, and flag-of-convenience abuse.
  • Chokepoint dependency: Suez, Hormuz, Bab al-Mandeb, Malacca, Bosporus — each a single point of failure.
  • Humanitarian consequences: Food security, especially in import-dependent developing countries.
  • Border weaponisation: Migration as a tool of pressure (Belarus-EU, etc.).

Committee 3 · 5 Delegations

Special Committee

The question of human rights and ethnic minorities in the Xinjiang region

This is the most politically sensitive topic of the conference. The committee will debate allegations concerning the treatment of Uyghurs, Kazakhs, and other Turkic Muslim minorities in China’s Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region. Expect a clear fracture: roughly 50 countries have signed UN letters criticising China’s policies, while a larger group has signed letters supporting China’s “counter-terrorism” framing. Approach with care — the topic touches on credible documented harm.

Note for delegates: This topic requires careful, diplomatic handling. Whatever position you represent, your job is to argue it credibly — not dismiss documented evidence or attack other delegates personally. Allegations include mass detention, forced labour, surveillance, and cultural restrictions; China’s framing emphasises counter-terrorism, vocational education, and poverty alleviation. A good delegate engages with the substance, even when the position is uncomfortable.

Topic Background

Why this topic, why now

Since around 2017, credible reports have documented large-scale detention facilities in Xinjiang, alongside expanded surveillance, restrictions on religious and cultural practice, and labour transfer programmes. China characterises its actions as counter-terrorism following violent incidents in the early 2010s, and points to economic development gains. The OHCHR’s 2022 assessment found possible “crimes against humanity”; China rejects the assessment as politicised. The topic now sits at the centre of a broader contest over the international human-rights system.

The dueling letters

In 2019 and again in 2022, two competing groups of states submitted letters to the UN Human Rights Council. Western and allied states criticised China’s Xinjiang policies; a larger group — including most Muslim-majority states, African states, and developing countries — signed letters supporting China’s position. This split is the central diplomatic reality of the debate.

Key tensions

  • Sovereignty vs. universal human rights: When does “internal affairs” end?
  • Counter-terrorism framing vs. cultural rights: Are the policies security measures or persecution?
  • Evidence and access: The OHCHR could not visit independently; satellite imagery, leaked documents, and survivor testimony form much of the public record.
  • Forced labour and global supply chains: The Uyghur Forced Labour Prevention Act (US) and similar measures have economic consequences.
  • The OIC paradox: Why have most Muslim-majority states sided with China?

Resolution Writing Quick Reference

Three parts of a resolution

  • Header: Committee, Topic, Sponsors, Signatories.
  • Preamble: Italicised clause openers, end in commas. Sets the scene, references past action.
  • Operatives: Underlined or italicised openers, numbered, end in semicolons (final clause ends in a full stop). State what the UN will do.

SMART operatives

Specific (who does what), Measurable (how do we know it worked), Achievable (within committee authority), Relevant (addresses the topic), Time-bound (deadlines or review periods).

Watch your committee’s authority

The General Assembly can recommend, encourage, request. It cannot demand, mandate, or condemn — those words are reserved for the Security Council. Using SC language in the GA is a procedural error and chairs will notice.

Three formatting traps that cost points

  • Repeating the same clause opener — use “Further” or “Strongly” to vary.
  • Forgetting to spell out acronyms on first use: World Health Organization (WHO).
  • Wrong punctuation: preamble clauses end in commas, operatives end in semicolons (final clause: full stop).

Public Speaking Reminders

Hook — Point — Call to Action

  • Hook: Question, quote, statistic, or short story. Grab attention in the first sentence.
  • Point: State your country’s position clearly. Give 2–3 reasons.
  • Call to Action: Propose a specific solution. End strong.

Speakers list vs. moderated caucus

The speakers list is general — about the topic as a whole, 1–2 minutes per speech. A moderated caucus is narrow — about a specific aspect of the topic, usually 30–60 seconds per speech. In a moderated caucus you can directly respond to other delegates; on the speakers list you make standalone arguments.

Decorum

Speak in third person about yourself: “The delegate of France believes…” not “I think…”. Refer to other delegates by country, not name. Stay diplomatic even when you disagree strongly.
